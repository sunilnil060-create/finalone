Photo Essay

Before the Mask

by Tshering Wangchuk and Pema Lhamo

Place: Haa, Bhutan

This series documents the hours before the public Tsechu ceremony begins — the quiet, restricted space of preparation where dancers, monks, and elders ready themselves for the ritual transformation that the cham dance requires.

Behind the dzong's thick walls, in a room that smells of camphor and aged silk, the cham dancers prepare in near silence. Robes are folded with deliberate care — each crease a form of reverence. Faces remain calm, almost blank, as if the act of stillness itself is the first movement of the dance.

The masks lie waiting on low wooden shelves: a wrathful deity with bulging eyes, a skull-crowned protector, a deer whose gentle expression belies its cosmic significance. The dancer who lifts his mask does so with both hands, pausing before placing it over his face. In that pause, something passes between the human and the sacred.

These photographs were made in the hours before the public ceremony begins, when access is restricted and the atmosphere is neither performance nor private — something in between, a threshold space. The dancers speak little. Instructions, when given, are relayed in a murmur. There is an economy to everything: of movement, of word, of breath.

What the camera found here was not spectacle but preparation — the quiet labour of transformation. The elaborate costumes, which will later fill the courtyard with colour and presence, are in this room still simply cloth: heavy, smelling of dust and incense, passed from hand to hand with the familiarity of objects that have been handled this way for generations.

The Tsechu at Haa is one of the oldest continuously performed festivals in western Bhutan. The dances enacted in the courtyard are not entertainment but scripture — stories of protection, of the vanquishing of evil, of the compassion of the enlightened. The dancers who carry these stories are not performers in the theatrical sense. They are vessels. The preparation room is where they become so.
